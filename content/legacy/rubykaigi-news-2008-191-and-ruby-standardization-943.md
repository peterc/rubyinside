<p><a href="http://jp.rubyist.net/RubyKaigi2008/english.html">RubyKaigi 2008</a> took place a couple of weeks ago. As the main Japanese Ruby conference, RubyKaigi is the de-facto <em>authoritative</em> Ruby conference, and the news that came out of the conference this year did little to shake its stature.</p>
<p>The online enterprise news publication InfoQ has covered the conference in <a href="http://www.infoq.com/news/2008/07/rubykaigi-interview-with-matz">two</a> <a href="http://www.infoq.com/news/2008/07/rubykaigi">parts</a>. The first features a mini interview with Matz, where he talks about the low adoption rate of Ruby in the enterprise and the role of certifications in the Ruby world. The <a href="http://www.infoq.com/news/2008/07/rubykaigi">second InfoQ post</a> reveals that Matz is preparing to "standardize" Ruby, with the ultimate aim to submit a Ruby standard to the <a href="http://www.iso.org/iso/home.htm">ISO</a>, and Koichi Sasada reveals that Ruby 1.9.1 (notable, as it will be the first officially stable / production-ready release of Ruby 1.9) is set to arrive this December.</p>
<p>A lot more seems to have gone down at RubyKaigi than this, as evidenced by the absolute <a href="http://del.icio.us/tag/rubykaigi">snowstorm of links tagged rubykaigi on del.icio.us</a>, though most of the evidence is in Japanese, of course.</p>
