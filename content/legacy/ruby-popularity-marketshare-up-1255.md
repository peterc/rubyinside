<p><img src="/assets/2008/10/chartgoesup.png" width="89" height="81" alt="chartgoesup.png" style="float:left; margin-right:12px; margin-bottom:6px;"> A week ago, in a story published by eWeek.com, Darryl K. Taft asked "<a href="http://www.eweek.com/c/a/Application-Development/Can-Ruby-Rails-Make-Developers-Shine-in-a-Downturn/">Can Ruby and Rails Make Developers Shine in a Downturn?</a>"</p>
<p>The general gist is that with a generally lower total cost of development, projects using Ruby and Rails will continue to increase, and even thrive in a recession, ensuring the success of Ruby and Rails developers. An analyst with Forrester, John Rymer, however, countered:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>I respectfully suggest that the <em>type</em> of application someone is working on is a more important factor than language usage. In a downturn, companies tend to go back to basics.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Whatever the final outcome, Ruby's popularity is still increasing. Dean Cruse of Five Runs <a href="http://blog.fiveruns.com/2008/10/8/ruby-on-rails-opportunity-in-a-financial-downturn">weighs in with his own opinion</a> - namely that "<em>Rails represents a low risk investment for the IT organization,</em>" due to its open source nature. Of 154 people polled in Dean's post, currently 44% see more opportunities for Rails developers heading into a recession, against only 15% who see "major cutbacks" ahead.</p>
<h3>Koders.com Sees 20x Increase in Ruby Searches</h3>
<p>Further, this week, Black Duck Software, the company behind the popular <a href="http://www.koders.com">Koders.com</a> search engine, has announced that it has seen a dramatic increase in the <a href="http://www.koders.com/stats">popularity of Ruby</a> on its code search site with the number of Ruby-related searches having increased 20 times since 2004. They've also announced that they're crawling more Ruby sources than ever, now including all of RubyForge. Tom Copeland, RubyForge's sys-admin, is delighted:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Black Duck’s search data confirms the tremendous growth that we are seeing within the community of Ruby developers. It’s great to see a leading code search site like Koders.com index RubyForge because it represents another way to make the projects in our community available to tens of thousands of developers worldwide.</p>
</blockquote>
<h3>4 million Rubyists by 2013?</h3>
<p>IT research company Gartner, Inc. are also convinced that Ruby's popularity is on the up, and the Ruby ecosystem will reach 4 million developers worldwide by 2013. Mark Driver, research VP at Gartner, Inc. says:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>[..] Ruby will enjoy a higher concentration among corporate IT developers than typical, dynamic ‘scripting’ languages, such as PHP.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>I'm not entirely buying this yet, but if it becomes true and Ruby leapfrogs PHP in the corporate environment, you could be looking at some pretty interesting years ahead as a Ruby developer!</p>
<h3>But Python Might Be Moving Faster..</h3>
<p>On the other hand, Ruby is still hovering at #10 in the <a href="http://www.tiobe.com/index.php/content/paperinfo/tpci/index.html">TIOBE Programming Community Index</a>, a position it's held for over a year now. While TIOBE's index isn't incredibly accurate, it makes a good indicator, and even <em>Delphi</em> has resurged back ahead of Ruby in the last year, and Python appears to be moving up the charts a lot quicker than Ruby.</p>
