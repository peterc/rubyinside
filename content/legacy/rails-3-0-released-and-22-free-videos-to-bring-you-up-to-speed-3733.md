<blockquote class="stylized"><p>Rails 3.0 has been underway for a good two years, so it’s with immense pleasure that we can declare it’s finally here. We’ve brought the work of more than 1,600 contributors together to make everything better, faster, cleaner, and more beautiful.</p></blockquote>
<p><cite>David Heinemeier Hansson</cite></p>
<p><img src="/assets/2010/02/rails3logo.gif" style="float: right; margin-left: 18px; margin-bottom: 18px; border: 1px solid #666">DHH rings the bell and announces that <a href="http://weblog.rubyonrails.org/2010/8/29/rails-3-0-it-s-done">Rails 3.0 (final) has been released</a> after two years of determined effort by the Rails core team (and, significantly, <a href="http://www.merbivore.com/">Merb</a> team members, since Rails 3.0 is heavily influenced by the <a href="http://rubyonrails.org/merb">Merb merger</a>). Grab it now with <code>gem install rails --version 3.0.0</code> or, if you're in no rush, Rails 3.0.1 might come along within a week or two.</p>

<h3>Or some books</h3>
<p>Michael Hartl's <a href="http://zfer.us/EKm97">Rails Tutorial book</a> is the #1 (and only, in my opinion) place to start when it comes to books about <em>learning</em> Rails 3.0. Not only is it available to read for free online, but you can buy a well formatted PDF too. It's an amazing piece of work and, unusually, walks you through building a Rails app from start to finish <em>with testing.</em> If you want to just read one book/site and feel like a Rails 3.0 master by the end of it, pick <a href="http://zfer.us/EKm97">RailsTutorial.org.</a></p>
<p><strong>Update:</strong> There are now also 15+ hours of <a href="http://zfer.us/EKm97">Rails Tutorial screencasts.</a> They're commercial but make for great viewing and are the best way to get an "over the shoulder" experience of how an experienced developer builds a Rails 3 from start to finish.</p>
<p>If you speak <strong>German</strong>, though, check out <a href="http://www.amazon.de/Ruby-Rails-DataMapper-Development-Volltextsuche/dp/3868020268/ref=pd_sxp_grid_i_0_2">this "Ruby on Rails 3" book</a> by Michael Voigt and Stefan Tennigkeit. It's one of the first Rails 3.0 specific books to hit the presses.</p>
<h3>Or just dive into some code</h3>
<p>If you want to just "get started" and check out a working Rails 3.0 application, try Daniel Kehoe's <a href="http://github.com/fortuity/rails3-subdomain-devise">Rails3-Subdomain-Devise app</a>. It's a basic Rails 3.0 app that demonstrates using the Devise authentication system, as well as custom subdomain access. Not just that, but Daniel has put together <a href="http://wiki.github.com/fortuity/rails3-subdomain-devise/tutorial-walkthrough">a walkthrough</a> of how the app works and how it was put together.</p>
